## Bucket 2 · Travelers hate (with frequency notes)

- **"Food-courtish" optics.** The Dukes Lane Market & Eatery setting is read by some reviewers as a food court before they sit down. One reviewer notes the building "looks food-courtish" before pivoting to the food being gourmet. The optics drag down trust on the dinner-occasion booking decision.
  Mentions: a recurring framing across TripAdvisor and OpenTable summaries in this sweep.
- **Outdoor seating next to hotel AC equipment and the parking garage.** "The outside seating is adjacent to hotel AC equipment and a parking garage." Sound and smell intrude on the patio diner expecting a Waikiki sit-down vibe.
  Mentions: Charles F, TripAdvisor, January 2025.
- **Service slips after the chef change.** "The eggs got left behind, the coffee whilst kind was cold." A separate reviewer flagged a prime rib temperature error ("ordered medium rare but was served very rare and cold") that the same diner had not experienced on a prior visit.
  Mentions: 2 across TripAdvisor 3-star and 2-star reviews in the last 12 months.
- **"The service was quite simply the worst we have experienced."** One 2-star outlier flagged a service collapse strong enough to make the headline. Single review, but the headline is the kind of thing a traveler skims on the TripAdvisor recent-reviews tab and bounces off.
  Mentions: 1, Connector635607, TripAdvisor, October 2024.
- **Happy-hour seating restriction.** "Previously we were able to sit outside but now happy hour is limited to seating at the bar." Travelers who associated Basalt with patio happy hour learned the policy at the door.
  Mentions: 1 verbatim (584brucen, TripAdvisor, December 2019). The bar-only happy-hour rule is still in effect on the current site, so the friction recurs on every group that does not read the fine print.
- **Cold prime rib at dinner.** Single negative on the dinner hero, but the dish is half the dinner-check anchor (alongside the charcoal pancake on the breakfast side), so a temperature miss is disproportionate.
  Mentions: 1, TripAdvisor 2024.

## Bucket 4 · What's changing in the category (industry context, last 90 days)

**Trend 1. Waikiki dine-in sales are down roughly 20% year-over-year, and the January 1, 2026 minimum-wage hike to $16 just landed.**
"It is the worst I've seen in about two years. Things have just slowed down significantly," Mike Palmer, Managing Partner of Kuhio Avenue Food Hall, told KITV in November 2025. Hawaiʻi Restaurant Association president Sheryl Matsuoka added "When the restaurant closes their doors, their employees lose a job." Restaurants that defend the check by stacking provenance + value levers are winning the share that is still there.
Source: https://www.kitv.com/news/waikiki-restaurants-struggle-as-tourism-lags-and-costs-rise/article_21da5d04-8936-4f21-ae00-f9c0a57f2af1.html

**Trend 2. Spend is up 14.8% YTD through February 2026 on arrivals up only 4%. Visitors are dining out more aggressively per trip even though fewer of them are coming.**
DBEDT reports $4.17B total visitor spending in the first two months of 2026, and explicitly calls out food and beverage as one of the largest growth categories across both U.S. East and Japanese visitor segments. The booking-page job is no longer to convert maximum arrivals; it is to convert maximum spend per visit.
Source: https://www.hawaii-guide.com/blog/hawaii-q1-2026-visitor-spending-surge-summer-impact

**Trend 3. The kamaʻāina lever is becoming a default expectation, not a marketing flourish.**
The Hawaiʻi Tribune-Herald reported in July 2025 that the visitor industry is "looking to isle residents to bolster business." Hotels are layering kamaʻāina rates, resort-fee waivers, and food-and-beverage credits. Restaurants are following. Basalt's 20% kamaʻāina discount was the restaurant's first ever (launched July 2025) and is already a regional signal of intent. The competitive question for 2026 is whether the brand makes the discount a recurring habit or treats it as a launch-window promotion.
Source: https://www.hawaiitribune-herald.com/2025/07/22/hawaii-news/hawaiis-visitor-industry-looks-to-isle-residents-to-bolster-business/

**Trend 4. The category's strongest direct comp on the pancake lane just exited the market.**
Cinnamon's at the ʻIlikai is now listed CLOSED on Yelp. Cinnamon's was the named guava-chiffon-pancake comp travelers used as the alternative to Basalt's charcoal pancake, with documented 1 to 2 hour Waikiki waits as recently as January 2025. Basalt has a six-month window where the closest comparable pancake destination has vanished from the Waikiki choice set. The window does not stay open forever.
Source: https://www.yelp.com/biz/cinnamons-at-the-ilikai-honolulu-4

## Three service-change recommendations (ranked by impact × effort)

### 1. Anchor the homepage and dinner-page hero on Lance Kosaka, the kamaʻāina discount, and the "we left the pancakes" story
- **Evidence:** The single biggest unaddressed anxiety in Bucket 3 is "is the food still good after the chef change," and the Kosaka resume is the most credible answer to it. The closest direct comp on the breakfast lane (Cinnamon's) just closed. The kamaʻāina discount was the restaurant's first in eight years and was launched in July 2025, deliberately, by an operator (ABC Stores) trying to seed local repeat. Right now the chef bio sits two clicks deep. Peak-end rule plus loss-aversion both argue for moving it to the homepage hero.
- **Where in the experience:** Pre-arrival (homepage + dinner page + OpenTable bio).
- **Category:** Communication / positioning.
- **Effort:** Low (copy + photo, no operational change).
- **Impact:** High (closes the chef-change anxiety, defends the dinner check, captures the kamaʻāina lever, claims the breakfast lane the closed Cinnamon's vacated).
- **Ship within 30 days:** Rewrite the homepage hero to lead with the line "17 years with Alan Wong. Now in the kitchen at Basalt. We changed almost everything except the pancakes." Stack a kamaʻāina-discount line and a "we left the prime rib and the pancakes" subhead under it. Replace the current generic interior shot with a Kosaka portrait above the pancake stack and a second shot of the prime rib at dinner. Make sure the homepage names that the restaurant is full-service with reservations, not a food-hall counter. Push the same three sentences into the OpenTable About copy and the Google Business Profile description so the chef-pedigree story shows up in AI-search answers too.

### 2. Stand up a "Pancake-and-Photo" guest moment with a watermarked digital shot delivered by email
- **Evidence:** The charcoal pancake is the eight-year visual hero dish that travelers photograph anyway. Honolulu Magazine described the stack as "volcanolike" with "the lava" (guava-strawberry purée) and "the plume" (mascarpone whipped cream). The plating is already the share moment. There is a YouTube tutorial on the brand channel where the chef teaches the recipe. There is no productized guest-photo capture, and the dinner-occasion comp set (Roy's, Duke's) does not run this either. Mere-exposure effect: turn the photo travelers already take into one the brand controls the frame on.
- **Where in the experience:** On-experience (plating, server beat, digital handoff).
- **Category:** Photo / share-the-moment.
- **Effort:** Mid (one trained host, one branded plate or printed card, one email-flow integration with the POS).
- **Impact:** High (drives organic social, lifts perceived value of the $20 pancake plate, gives the brand a first-party email capture out of every walk-in table that walks out otherwise unnamed).
- **Ship within 30 days:** Train the server team to offer "I can take the pancake shot for you, would you like it texted or emailed when you get home?" at the beat the plate hits the table. Print a small "@basaltwaikiki" tent card with the plate. Stand up a Zapier or Lightspeed integration that pushes a watermarked digital file to the diner's email within 24 hours of the meal. No print add-on or photo-package upsell in the first 30 days; prove the email-capture lift first.

### 3. Stack the Waikiki Walk-Up bundle: 20% kamaʻāina + happy-hour bar value + parking validation as one bookable offer
- **Evidence:** Three structural advantages are sitting on the site in three separate places. The 20% kamaʻāina discount is the strongest local-trust signal in Waikiki. The $5 wagyu happy-hour burger is named by Frolic Hawaiʻi as best in Waikiki. The Hyatt Centric self-parking validation is mentioned on the contact page but not on the homepage. The Bucket 4 spend-data trend (visitors spending more per trip) plus the dine-in-decline trend together say the restaurant's job in 2026 is to convert higher spend per booking on a smaller cover count. Pratfall effect: name the trade-off (yes the bar-only happy-hour rule exists, here's why, here's what you still get) and the friction stops feeling like a gotcha.
- **Where in the experience:** Pre-arrival (homepage + booking page + confirmation email) + on-experience (server framing).
- **Category:** Pricing + cadence + communication.
- **Impact:** Mid (defends the dinner check, captures kamaʻāina repeat-visit, claims the local-value lane Cinnamon's vacated and Goofy / Heavenly own loosely).
- **Effort:** Mid (no operational change; reposition existing offers as one stacked bundle on the site).
- **Ship within 30 days:** Add a "Walk-Up from the Hyatt Centric" block to the homepage and the contact page that names three things in one place: 20% kamaʻāina discount (rules + hours + ID required), bar-seating happy-hour 2pm to 5pm with the $5 wagyu burger callout, complimentary self-parking validation. Pair with a single sentence: "Three hours of parking, happy-hour pricing at the bar, and a kamaʻāina rate if you live here. We made the walk-up worth it." Push the same bundle into the OpenTable Special Offers block and into the confirmation-email template so it lands as care, not as a hidden charge.
