Signal 1, FAQ gaps

Questions guests ask that the website doesn't answer

Did the Charcoal Buttermilk Pancakes survive the 2025 menu redesign?
They did. Press coverage confirms Chef Kosaka "changed mostly everything except the pancakes." But the homepage never mentions the pancake by name and never confirms it stayed. A returning guest has to trust a 2018 TripAdvisor review or a 2025 Honolulu Magazine piece. The hero dish is invisible at the booking moment.
How do I actually find Basalt inside Dukes Lane Market & Eatery?
Multiple reviewers and bloggers warn the entrance is easy to miss and the path runs past the burger and gelato counters. The contact page lists an address only. Wayfinding is owned by third parties, which costs the operator first-time bookings.
Where exactly does the 20% kamaʻāina discount apply?
The homepage carousel shows a "kamaʻāina discount" tile but does not state the redemption rule. Does it apply at breakfast, dinner, happy hour, takeout, with a valid Hawaiʻi ID, on the full check or food only? A local diner cannot answer this before clicking through, so the offer underconverts.
Is dinner a $60 night or a $200 night?
Press lists dinner entrées at $30 to $45 and reports check averages of "$100+ per person." Nothing on the homepage frames the expected check, which leaves the dominant anxiety from the third-party listings unanswered.
What is the cancellation policy if I book on OpenTable?
Not visible on the homepage or contact page. Standard OpenTable rules apply by default, but a single line beside the reservation button removes the "I'll look elsewhere" reflex.
Signal 2, Comp parity

Where you stack up against Roy's Waikiki, Goofy Cafe, and MW Restaurant

Themes you own

TripAdvisor rank #19 of 1,749 Honolulu restaurants.Roy's Waikiki sits at #73 with 3,569 reviews. MW Restaurant at #118 with 341 reviews. Basalt wins on ranking but does not surface the rank anywhere on the homepage.
A signature dish the brand can stand on.Charcoal Buttermilk Pancakes have eight years of Hawaiʻi press behind them. Roy's, Goofy, and MW each have menus but no single dish that diners name unprompted at the same frequency.
Real chef pedigree (Lance Kosaka, 17 years with Alan Wong).Same lineage as MW's Wade Ueoka and Michelle Karr-Ueoka. MW makes that pedigree the homepage. Basalt buries it on a chef bio page.
Four dayparts under one roof, with a $5 to $6 happy hour in Waikiki.Roy's, Goofy, MW each cover at most two dayparts. Basalt's 2pm to 5pm bar pricing is a genuine value play that none of the three comps reproduce.

Themes they own that you don't

Review volume as a trust signal.Roy's Waikiki: 6,797 OpenTable + 3,569 TripAdvisor. Goofy: 3,886 Yelp + 1,811 TripAdvisor. MW: 3,525 OpenTable. Basalt: 2,218 OpenTable + 445 TripAdvisor. Wins on quality, loses on volume, and the booking page does not surface the contrast.
Embedded booking widget on the homepage.Goofy Cafe and MW route directly through OpenTable from the homepage button. Basalt's "Make a Reservation" pops to an external OpenTable URL with no in-page widget, which adds a click and breaks the booking momentum.
A clear "what to expect" line on the booking moment.Roy's and MW frame the check average and the dress code on the reservation page. Basalt's homepage states hours and parking, but says nothing about price band, attire, or wayfinding before the OpenTable click.
Wayfinding owned by the brand, not by third-party blogs.Goofy and MW sit in stand-alone buildings, so wayfinding is trivial. Basalt sits inside a food hall and writes about itself the same way, which forces guests to learn the path from a Star Advertiser dining column.
