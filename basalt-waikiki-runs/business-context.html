Business Overview

  • Name: Basalt Waikiki (sometimes styled BASALT in press).
  • One-liner: "Waikiki's Hidden Gem". A locally inspired, globally influenced comfort-food restaurant and bar tucked inside Dukes Lane Market & Eatery on the ground floor of the Hyatt Centric Waikiki Beach Hotel.
  • Category: Restaurant-as-experience. New American with Hawaiʻi-regional accents, plus a happy-hour bar program. Breakfast, brunch, lunch, dinner under one roof.
  • Location: Oʻahu, Waikiki. 2255 Kūhiō Ave, Honolulu, HI 96815. Corner of Dukes Lane and Kūhiō, inside Dukes Lane Market & Eatery at the Hyatt Centric Waikiki Beach Hotel.
  • Operating model: Single-location, full-service restaurant + bar. Operated under the ABC Stores umbrella (Paul Kosasa's family company), one of seven F&B concepts inside the Dukes Lane food-hall format. Breakfast 7am–1pm, lunch 11am–1pm, happy hour (bar only) 2pm–5pm, dinner 5pm–9pm. Closed Tuesdays and Wednesdays as of January 20, 2026.
  • Group size: Not visible on site. Refine to fill. (Large-party special is promoted, suggesting groups are actively courted.)
  • Pricing tier: Mid to upper-mid. Breakfast $20–22 per plate, dinner entrées $30–45, appetizers $7–22, desserts $12. Dinner check averages reported around $100+ per person in third-party listings.
  • Year-round / seasonal: Year-round. Closed Tue/Wed.

Signature Experiences

  • Charcoal Buttermilk Pancakes with guava-strawberry sauce. The hero dish. Press has covered it from 2018 (Honolulu Magazine, Frolic Hawaii) through the 2025 menu revamp. Kept on the menu when Chef Lance Kosaka changed "mostly everything except the pancakes." Retail pancake mix is sold next door at Dukes Lane Market and online.
  • Happy Hour at the bar. 2pm–5pm. Repeatedly cited in reviews and press (Frolic Hawaii "best happy hour burger," Hawaiʻi News Now 3-part happy hour series, Star Advertiser "Pau Hana Patrol"). Strong value perception in a strip where most Waikiki bars are tourist-priced.
  • Prime Rib at dinner. The other survivor of the 2025 menu redesign. Anchors the dinner check.
  • Brunch program post-redesign. Citrus-cured salmon toast ($22), pork hash loco moco ($22), beef stew shakshuka ($24), biscuit sandwich ($20). Kosaka's reframe of breakfast as a destination meal, not a quick eat.
  • Salmon chazuke risotto and guava baby back ribs. Two of Chef Kosaka's signature 2025 introductions. Hawaiʻi-regional flavors in a modern New American frame.

Ideal Guest

Inferred from review tone, press coverage, and pricing structure:

  • Mainland US visitor staying at the Hyatt Centric or nearby Kūhiō Ave hotels who wants a sit-down breakfast that isn't the in-house hotel buffet, and a dinner spot that isn't a chain. Walk-up convenience is the unfair advantage.
  • Couples and small groups. TripAdvisor data flags couples and friends as the dominant traveler type.
  • Kamaʻāina diners. The 20% kamaʻāina discount launched July 2025 was the restaurant's first ever, and it signals the operator wants locals back. Lance Kosaka is from Honolulu, Iolani / UH grad, ex-Alan Wong, ex-Mariposa, ex-53 By The Sea: he reads as a local-chef pick to the kamaʻāina audience.
  • Happy-hour seekers. Locals and visitors looking for a Waikiki bar that isn't a beachfront tourist trap; the 2pm–5pm slot is a genuine value play.
  • Foodies tracking the Honolulu chef circuit. Press traffic from Honolulu Magazine, Frolic, Star Advertiser, and Hawaiʻi News Now keeps Basalt visible to that audience.

What the Trip is Really For

"We're in Waikiki for a week. We don't want to eat at the hotel restaurant every day and we don't want to fight a 45-minute wait at Cinnamon's for pancakes. We found Basalt. Sit-down, real chef, kamaʻāina discount, walk to it from the hotel, and the charcoal pancakes are the photo we send home."

  • Anxieties guests carry into the booking: Is it really a restaurant or is it a food-hall stall? Is it loud (the property sits next to the parking garage)? Is dinner a $200 night or a $60 night? Is the menu still good after the 2025 chef change? Can we get in without a reservation? Is the happy hour actually happy hour pricing or tourist pricing?
  • The "miss" if they don't book: They eat hotel buffet eggs and a tourist-strip dinner. They miss the one sit-down on Kūhiō Ave that has eight years of Hawaiʻi press behind it and a chef with a 17-year Alan Wong pedigree. The charcoal pancake photo doesn't happen.

  • Goofy Cafe & Dine. https://goofy-cafe.com (Ena Rd, locavore breakfast, surfer-vibe, farm-to-table)
  • Heavenly Island Lifestyle. https://www.heavenly-waikiki.com (Shoreline Hotel Waikiki, locally sourced, popular with Japanese visitors)
  • Island Vintage Wine Bar. https://www.islandvintagecoffee.com (Royal Hawaiian Center, all-day breakfast wine bar, açaí bowls)

Adjacent (different category, same Waikiki dinner / happy-hour slot):

  • Roy's Waikiki. Hawaiʻi-regional dinner, same trip slot, higher price point.
  • Tommy Bahama Restaurant & Bar Waikiki. Bar-forward, tourist happy hour, Kalākaua location.
  • Duke's Waikiki. Iconic beachfront, completely different vibe, same dinner-occasion slot.

Differentiation

The two homepage moves doing the comp-set work:

  1. Charcoal pancakes as a single recognizable hero dish. Eight years of Hawaiʻi press built one signature item the brand can stand on. Most Waikiki restaurants don't have a single dish they own; Basalt does.
  2. Real chef pedigree inside a food-hall location. Lance Kosaka (17 years with Alan Wong, plus Mariposa, 53 By The Sea, Sky Waikiki) does not normally cook inside a market food-court setup. The pairing is the wedge. ABC Stores gave him carte blanche to redesign the menu in 2025; that is a serious operator commitment to chef-driven cooking in a hotel-ground-floor space.

Why Travelers Pick You (Four Forces)

  • Push: Frustration with the in-hotel buffet, with tourist-strip dinners on Kalākaua, and with 45-minute pancake waits at Cinnamon's. Frustration with food-hall stalls that don't have table service.
  • Pull: A full sit-down restaurant on the ground floor of Hyatt Centric Waikiki with a recognizable signature dish, a chef with a real Honolulu resume, a 2pm–5pm happy hour, and a 20% kamaʻāina discount. Walking distance from the heart of Waikiki without crossing Kalākaua.
  • Habit: Travelers default to OpenTable and to "best breakfast in Waikiki" listicles. Basalt appears on OpenTable (4.7 of 5, 2,218+ reviews) and on most listicles, but the booking habit pulls them off-site. The website still routes most reservations through the phone or OpenTable, not a direct booking widget on the homepage.
  • Anxiety: Is the new chef as good as the press says? Is the food-hall location really a real restaurant? Will the bill be $60 or $200? Will the pancakes still be there after the menu redesign (they are, but the homepage does not address this directly)?

Voice. How the Brand Sounds

Adjectives: Warm, casual, locally rooted, photo-forward, food-as-gathering, low on chef-ego, light on jargon, "hidden gem" framing.

Words the brand uses:

  • "Hidden gem"
  • "Locally inspired, globally influenced"
  • "Hawaiʻi regional cuisine"
  • "Aloha spirit service"
  • "A gathering place for friends, family, and food enthusiasts"
  • "Food brings people together"

Words/clichés the brand avoids: "Fine dining," "luxury," "world-class," "exclusive," "VIP." The voice is deliberately not chef-as-auteur and not white-tablecloth, even though the chef's resume could support either positioning.

Sample sentence from the site:

"We believe that food brings people together."
